Electric Cars in France: Pushing Forward into the Future

Electric vehicles are becoming a necessary component of the automotive industry, no longer just a futuristic idea. France is moving toward greener mobility as the country's network of charging stations grows and the number of electric cars on the road rises. But what obstacles still need to be overcome and how far have we come? Let's examine the data and predict what lies ahead.

Adoption of Electric Vehicles: Where Do We Stand?

In France, the adoption of electric vehicles has gained momentum. As of right now, 2.20% of all vehicles are electric. Even with this year's notable increase, it is evident that the percentage of cars on the road that are electric still remains small. France has increased the number of charging stations in its network to facilitate this shift.

Today, there are 99,952 public charging points across the country. However, these stations vary greatly in their capacity:

The most powerful charging station offers up to 400 kW, while the weakest operates at 10 kW, showing a wide variability in charging speeds with a standard deviation of 83 kW. The European standard Combo CCS plugs are found in 26.1% of stations, whereas CHAdeMO connectors are present in 19.63% of stations. This illustrates the challenges of creating uniformity in charging options to satisfy the increasing demands of electric vehicle owners.

The growth of electric vehicles faces challenges not only in the number of charging stations but also in their efficiency. The average power of charging stations in France is 57 kW, which is still below the rapid-charging expectations of many drivers.

Moreover, the separation of different connector types implies the need for additional standardization. Even though Combo CCS is leading the market, CHAdeMO is still present, suggesting that a change is still happening. It will cost money to bring the network together and make every car more generally compatible.

France needs to prioritize enhancing its charging infrastructure if it hopes to maintain the rise in the number of electric vehicles on the road. This means it's important to invest in faster charging stations in order to shorten charging times.

The transition to electric vehicles in France is well started, but there is still a long road ahead. The key to realizing the full potential of electric transportation is sustained infrastructure growth and standardization. The future appears bright with new policies and public investments taking form. The moment has come to take advantage of this and move France in the direction of entirely electric vehicles in the future.
